Kelp Value and Threats

Assessment and Protection of Kelp Project — An initial compilation of scientific understanding related to kelp ecology, benefits, and anthropogenic threats in San Juan County

Published 2007 · San Juan County, Washington

Introduction

This document represents an initial compilation of scientific understanding related to the ecological function, benefits and anthropogenic threats for understory and floating kelps in San Juan County, Washington.

The objective of the report is to provide a summary of known scientific information on kelps to inform relevant planning processes including the San Juan County Critical Areas Ordinance Update, Marine Stewardship Area Strategic Plan and Shoreline Master Program Update.

Legal Protections

Kelps are protected under the Environmentally Sensitive Areas section of the San Juan County Unified Development Code (SJCC 18.30.110.5) and are listed as a priority habitat type by the Department of Fish and Wildlife's Priority Habitats and Species Program.

Washington State Administrative Code (WAC 220-110-250.1.d and e) also specifically protects kelps used as spawning substrate by Pacific herring and as settlement/nursery grounds for juvenile rockfish.

Ecological Function

Kelp is defined as benthic brown algae in the order Laminariales (Duggins et al. 2003, Gabrielson 2006). In lay terms, most large brown algae attached to the seabed in this region are kelps.

Understory kelps grow to a meter or two in length on rocky substrate from the intertidal to depths of approximately 45 ft (~14 m). Bull kelp attach to rock with a holdfast usually in 10–30 ft (~3–9 m) water depths to a maximum of 80 ft (~24 m), and grow to the surface with a gas-filled float which holds the blades where energy from the sun for photosynthesis is greatest.

Life History

The life history of kelp species includes alternation of generations, with the large plant (sporophyte) producing planktonic spores which remain in the water column for a period of time before settling onto the bottom (Mumford 2006). Once on the bottom, the spores germinate into small plants (male and female gametophytes) that require certain environmental conditions to produce eggs or sperm which fertilize then grow quickly into the large kelp plant.

Kelp Value and Benefits

Kelps provide many ecosystem services, such as primary ecosystem productivity as well as habitat for many organisms, including marine mammals, fish and invertebrates (Dayton 1985, Steneck et al. 2002).

Key Ecosystem Services

Carbon cycling: Kelp-derived carbon is found throughout nearshore food webs, and growth rates of benthic suspension feeders increase in the presence of organic detritus originating from kelps (Duggins et al. 1989). Many invertebrates in San Juan County rely on drift algae as a food supply, especially urchins.

Physical habitat: Understory kelps provide habitat structure on the seabed, including reduced fluid transport near the bottom, greater rates of particulate deposition and penetration of suspended particles from the water column to the bottom (Eckman et al. 1989).

Wave dampening: Kelp dampens waves and alters the energy regime on beaches, influencing beach sediment grain size and sediment–biotic suitability such as suitability for beach spawners.

Salmon & Fish Habitat

Salmon use kelp habitats. Simenstad et al. (1979) found chinook, coho, and chum salmon in kelp beds of the Strait of Juan de Fuca. Murphy et al. (2000) found greater densities of seven species of salmonids in habitat with kelp than without in Southeast Alaska. A similar study by Dean et al. (2000) found sculpins and rockfish primarily associated with kelps.

In the Strait of Juan de Fuca, Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife research found that juvenile salmon and surf smelt, an important forage fish, preferentially utilized kelp bed habitats (Shaffer 2003).

Juvenile Fish Habitat

Kelp habitats are very important to juvenile fishes. Young-of-the-year copper and quillback rockfish strongly associate with understory kelp (Hayden-Spear 2006). Bull kelp forests are especially important habitat for very young copper rockfish that settle into shallow reef habitats from the plankton (Haldorson and Richards 1987). These fish eventually migrate down plants to the reef habitat (Buckley 1997).

Leaman (1980) documented extensive use of kelp habitat by fishes in Barkley Sound, British Columbia and found a higher diversity and abundance of fishes in kelp beds than elsewhere.

Threats

Kelp faces multiple anthropogenic and environmental threats in San Juan County waters, ranging from sedimentation and development to climate change and invasive species.

Sedimentation

Inhibits spore settlement, reduces light, smothers young plants, and buries rocky substrate. Caused by poor land use practices and boat wakes.

Freshwater Inputs

Kelp exposed to freshwater for a week shows tissue deterioration. Bull kelp develops blisters from rapid salinity reductions.

Climate Change

The 1997 El Niño caused a 75% reduction in bull kelp bed size on the outer coast. Warming reduces upwelling and increases temperatures.

Over-water Structures

Docks and other structures reduce light reaching the seabed, reducing kelp and the prey resources that juvenile salmon depend on.

Petroleum Products

Diesel fuel, intermediate fuel oil, and crude oil all cause direct tissue damage and greatly reduce photosynthesis rates in bull kelp.

Invasive Species

Sargassum muticum, introduced from Japan in the 1940s, reduces kelp abundance primarily through shading competition.

Sedimentation

Sedimentation impacts kelp in two primary ways. First, it affects the microscopic kelp life history stages by inhibiting settlement of spores, reducing light availability in the water column and smothering gametophytes and young sporophytes after they have settled (Mumford 2006, Britton-Simmons pers. com., Duggins pers. com.). Suspended sediments also reduce the amount of light available to the plants, reducing growth rates and eliminating the plants in the lowest regions.

In the second primary impact, large inputs of sediment can reduce the rocky habitat substrate available for kelps by directly burying it. Studies have shown reduced bull kelp density in areas associated with landslides (Shaffer and Parks 1994).

Climate Change

Documented Impact

Annual surveys conducted by the Washington Department of Natural Resources since 1989 showed a 75% reduction in bed size for bull kelp on the outer coast during the 1997 El Niño event (Berry et al. 2001). El Niño events also cause less coastal upwelling, which stratifies the upper water column, causing lower nutrient conditions and high temperatures.

However, elevated atmospheric carbon dioxide appears to increase the photosynthetic rate of bull kelp (Thom 1996).

Over-water Structures

Over-water structures reduce benthic vegetation, such as kelp, presumably due to reduction in light energy reaching the benthos. Less kelp thereby reduces epibenthic prey resources which use this vegetation for habitat and are prey items for juvenile salmon (Haas 2002). The overall effect is reduced prey availability for juvenile salmon under over-water structures, such as docks.

Invasive Species

The invasive alga Sargassum muticum, introduced to Puget Sound from Japan in the 1940s (Giver 1999), poses a significant threat to kelp habitat. In a removal experiment in the San Juan Islands, kelp was more abundant in plots without S. muticum (Britton-Simmons 2004). The most abundant brown alga (Saccharina subsimplex) grew more than twice as fast in plots where the invasive species was absent. The negative effect appears to be shading, rather than changes in water flow, sedimentation or nutrient availability.

Harvesting

Kelps are harvested for many reasons, including human consumption, fertilizer production, and for feed at aquaculture farms (Kalvass et al. 2004). Harvest of kelp and all other seaweeds is regulated by law (RCW 77.32.520, 79.135.400-430). Most harvest of kelp in Washington State is associated with the harvest of herring roe, primarily in mariculture facilities in the Strait of Juan de Fuca and other locations outside of San Juan County.

Recreational harvest allows for collection of up to 10 pounds of algae (wet weight) per person per day. While limited experimental harvest in British Columbia showed no significant effects on recruitment (Foreman 1984), another study found that harvest could negatively influence both canopy biomass available as habitat and recruitment to the bed in subsequent years (Roland 1985).

Grazing & Predator Dynamics

Fishery removals of grazers by humans, especially urchins and abalone, affect kelp abundance by reducing grazing. Population fluctuations in predators of these invertebrates, such as sea otters, also cause fluctuations in kelp density (Vanblaricom and Estes 1988).

Monitoring

Researchers with the Washington Department of Natural Resources Nearshore Habitat Program have inventoried canopy-forming kelp distribution since 1989 (Berry et al. 2005). Between 1994 and 2000, DNR characterized all Washington State shorelines through the ShoreZone inventory, which included categories for both understory and canopy kelp habitat distribution (Berry et al. 2001). Much less is known about the status and trends of understory kelp because it is more difficult to monitor and historical information is not available.
